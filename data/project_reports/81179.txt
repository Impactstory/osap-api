<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Goals and Objectives
 </div></div>
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
Neuropeptides--which act as so-called 'slow transmitters' at neuronal synapses--are highly specific regulators of stress responses in humans and other mammals. The overall goals of the Section on Molecular Neuroscience are to i)identify the neuropeptide-regulated genes that mediate psychological and physical stress responses in vivo and ii) find the unique and specific cellular signaling components that allow neuropeptides to control these genes.  The linkage between stress-related signaling, and the gene transcription required for neuronal protection against traumatic effects of stress, will ultimately be established by examining a specific example of stress-related signaling from first messenger to gene activation, drilling down to uncover the biochemical steps connecting the two, and developing specific pharmacological reagents to appropriately block or stimulate those steps for therapeutic effect in vivo. The pituitary adenylate cyclase-activating polypeptide (PACAP) is a neuropeptide slow transmitter that elevates post-synaptic cyclic AMP (through Gs) and calcium (through Gq and other mechanisms) after secretion from pre-synaptic neurons. PACAP-deficient mice show impaired adrenomedullary responses to hypoglycemia, and neuroprotective responses to brain ischemia (Hamelink et al. PNAS 99:461,2002; Chen et al., Regul. Peptides 137:4, 2006), as well as specific impairment of stress activation of the HPA axis (Stroth and Eiden Neuroscience 165: 1025, 2010; Stroth et al., J. Neuroendocrinol. 23:944, 2011). We recently discovered that PACAP is capable of enhancing transcription of specific target genes in neuroendocrine cells through a cyclic AMP-dependent mechanism that, importantly, does not require the participation of protein kinase A (PKA)(Ravni et al., Mol. Pharmacol. 73:1688, 2008; Holighaus, Mustafa and Eiden, Peptides 32:1647, 2011; Emery and Eiden, FASEB J 26:3199, 2012). This observation allows expansion of the current view that all cAMP signaling to the nucleus is PKA-dependent, and suggests the need for a new pharmacology for cAMP signaling in the nervous system. The signaling components connecting cAMP to the nucleus in this pathway are being characterized by our laboratory, and contain the guanine nucleotide exchange factor NCS/Rapgef2, now appreciated to be a novel cAMP sensor in neurons and endocrine cells (Emery, Eiden, V., Mustafa and Eiden, L., SCIENCE SIGNALING 6 (281), ra51, 2013). Furthermore, the very existence of this pathway indicates the need to sunset the paradigm that only PKA-mediated signaling affect gene expression in the CNS, and to investigate in detail the relative involvement of PKA, Rapgefs 3 and 4 (so-called Epacs), and NCS/Rapgef2 (see Emery and Eiden, FASEB J 26:3199,2012) in neuronal signaling. Clearly, full delineation of the molecular pathways involved in cAMP-mediated neuronal responses to psychological and systemic stress and tissue injury will enhance development of innovative pharmacological therapeutic strategies to treat stress-associated and neurogenerative disorders that require signaling through cAMP. Our immediate aims are two-fold. First, we will complete the molecular and pharmacological characterization of the cAMP sensor through which genes responsive to cAMP signaling are uniquely activated by slow transmitters such as PACAP in both central and peripheral PACAP-responsive neurons. Second, we will determine the physiological relevance of this novel signaling system to synaptic transmission and cellular plasticity in hypothalamus, amygdala, hippocampus and cerebral cortex in response to depressogenic and non-depressogenic stress, minor traumatic brain injury, and learning involved in processing of aversive stimuli.

<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Summary
 </div></div>
 
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
The neuropeptide slow transmitter PACAP (pituitary adenylate cyclase activating polypeptide) is released at synapses that transduce stress responses to the brain, and mediate homeostatic adjustments to stress by the organism. Allostatic responses to systemic and psychogenic stressors at multiple points in development and throughout the life span are implicated as causative factors in depression and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Stress response pathways (resilience responses) may also be required to ameliorate delayed neuronal death (DND) in traumatic brain injury, long-term exposure to intense physical or psychological stimuli, or brain inflammation in chronic neurodegenerative disease. Understanding the cellular mechanisms of stress transduction is crucial to developing effective therapeutic interventions for these disorders.<br /><br />We recently completed and published, with our collaborators Eberhard Weihe (Marburg University), Greti Aguilera (NIH), and Corey Smith  (Case Western Reserve), experiments demonstrating that PACAP release at the adrenomedullary synapse is required for acute and chronic systemic and psychogenic stress responses, while in the PACAP is required specifically for psychogenic stress responses (Stroth and Eiden, Neuroscience 165:1025, 2010; Stroth et al., J. Neuroendocrinol. 23:944, 2011; Stroth et al., Endocrinology 154: 330, 2013). PACAP-dependent activation of neuroprotective proteins accompanies prolonged stress at these anatomical locations, suggesting a second role for PACAP in limiting cellular damage accompanying overstimulation of stress-transducing cells. <br /><br />PACAP activates several genes in cultured bovine chromaffin cells which are also regulated by PACAP during stress in vivo, including those encoding steroidogenic and neuroprotective secreted peptides, such as galanin, stanniocalcin, and VIP. This induction is dependent on a novel cAMP-dependent, PKA-independent signaling pathway different from the one required for cAMP- and calcium-dependent effects on memory and learning. We have now characterized this signaling pathway so that pharmacological interventions in stress relevant to mental health might be developed that do not at the same time interfere with normal processes of memory and learning that depend on cyclic AMP. We have also characterized the induction of the neuro- and cardio-protective gene product stanniocalcin as a PACAP-regulated gene in a neuroblastoma-glioma cell line, and in cultured rat cortical neurons, and demonstrated that this pathway is also a cAMP-dependent, PKA-independent one (Holighaus, Mustafa and Eiden, Peptides 32:1647, 2011; Holighaus, Weihe and Eiden, J. Mol. Neurosci. 46:75, 2011). <br /><br />Recently, we have developed in collaboration with members of the Section on Functional Neuroanatomy, NIMH-IRP, behavioral assays for monitoring the role of PACAP in depressive behaviors generated by prolonged social stress. These studies suggest a role for PACAP in extrahypothalamic stress responses as well as in the regulation of the HPA axis (Lehmann et al., Pshcyoneuroendocrinology 38: 702, 2013).
