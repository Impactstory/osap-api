<div class="headings">
Goals and Objectives
 </div></div>
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
The objective of this project is to provide the means to determine the fundamental molecular agents of neuronal activity -what makes a neuronal circuit functional? How does the neuron adapt to learning and memory on the molecular level? Answers to these questions require the means to parse the complexity of the cell molecule-by-molecule, a task for which the technique of mass spectrometry is well matched. Mass spectrometers are analytical tools that determine the molecular mass of a compound, and provide a means to characterize the architecture of the elemental assembly of a molecule. Intact proteins are generally too massive and fragile to be separated and analyzed directly, as they are assembled to fold and to be stabilized by interactions with neighboring proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, and water. Among the many types of mass spectrometers are instruments engineered for characterization of sub-units of proteins, or peptides, that can be specifically generated enzymatically. By accurately identifying and quantifying peptides, it is possible to reconstruct the framework and environment of proteins within the cell. Our goal is to develop methods in mass spectrometry and ancillary tools to reveal accurate and precise information about the molecular architecture of normal and abnormal subcellular organelles in neurons. A combination of new chemical, molecular biological, immunological, and physical separation techniques are required to understand the intricate molecular interaction of functional sub-cellular units, genetically coded and comprised principally of proteins in highly ordered assemblies. The identity of individual protein units, their relative stoichiometry in organelles, and the identity of post-translational modifications that influence organellar functional activity are major challenges in unraveling how synapses function, as well as the effects of psychotropic drugs on synapses.
</div>

<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Summary
 </div></div>
 
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
Mass spectrometric methods for the analysis of protein structures are being improved, applied and tested in independent and collaborative research projects in proteomics. Projects in separation methods and data processing are in progress. In the past year, progress has been made on implementation of a computational environment for mass spectrometric proteomic data processing; isotope labeled fusion proteins as protein standards; quantification of identified and unidentified components in sets of complex liquid chromatography-high resolution mass spectrometry data;  and multi-institutional collaborative studies on standards in proteomics. <br />The processing and mining of mass spectrometric proteomic data requires a system allowing investigators desktop computer access to terabytes of data; the ability to repeatedly probe their data with emerging software tools; and the ability to transfer this data to public archival repositories.  A versatile system has been implemented in LNT and will be shared with other proteomics groups in NIH.<br />
</div>
