<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Goals and Objectives
 </div></div>
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
Neuropeptides--which act as so-called 'slow transmitters' at neuronal synapses--are highly specific regulators of stress responses in humans and other mammals. The overall goals of the Section on Molecular Neuroscience are to identify the neuropeptide-regulated genes that mediate psychological and physical stress responses in vivo, and the unique and specific cellular signaling components that allow neuropeptides to control these genes.  The linkage between stress-related signaling, and the gene transcription required for neuronal protection against traumatic effects of stress, will ultimately be established by examining a specific example of stress-related signaling from first messenger to gene activation, drilling down to uncover the biochemical steps connecting the two, and developing specific pharmacological reagents to appropriately block or stimulate those steps for therapeutic effect in vivo. The pituitary adenylate cyclase-activating polypeptide (PACAP) is a neuropeptide slow transmitter that elevates post-synaptic cyclic AMP (through Gs) and calcium (through Gq and other mechanisms) after secretion from pre-synaptic neurons. PACAP-deficient mice show impaired adrenomedullary responses to hypoglycemia, and neuroprotective responses to brain ischemia (Hamelink et al. PNAS 99:461,2002; Chen et al., Regul. Peptides 137:4, 2006), as well as specific impairment of stress activation of the HPA axis (Stroth and Eiden Neuroscience 165: 1025, 2010; Stroth et al., J. Neuroendocrinol. 23:944, 2011). PACAP enhances transcription of specific target genes in neuroendocrine cells through a cyclic AMP-dependent mechanism that does not require the participation of protein kinase A (PKA)(Ravni et al., Mol. Pharmacol. 73:1688, 2008; Holighaus, Mustafa and Eiden, Peptides 32:1647, 2011; Emery and Eiden, FASEB J 26:3199, 2012). This observation allows expansion of the current view that all cAMP signaling to the nucleus is PKA-dependent, and suggests the need for a new pharmacology for cAMP signaling in the nervous system. The signaling component that uniquely connects cAMP elevation via GPCR ligand occupancy, to activation of the MAP kinase ERK, in neuronal and endocrine cells, has been identified in our laboratory. It is the neuritogenic cAMP sensor (NCS), which is the protein product of the Rapgef2 gene, termed by us NCS/Rapgef2 (Emery, Eiden, V., Mustafa and Eiden, L., SCIENCE SIGNALING 6 (281), ra51, 2013). The existence of this pathway inaugurates the sunsetting of the paradigm that only PKA-mediated signaling affect gene expression in the CNS, and impels our laboratory to investigate in detail the relative involvement of PKA, Rapgefs 3 and 4 (so-called Epacs), and NCS/Rapgef2 (see Emery and Eiden, FASEB J 26:3199,2012) in neuronal signaling leading to the cellular plasticity underlying learning, memory, and adaptation to stress. Full delineation of the molecular pathways involved in cAMP-mediated neuronal responses to psychological and systemic stress and tissue injury will enhance development of innovative pharmacological therapeutic strategies to treat stress-associated and neurogenerative disorders that require signaling through cAMP. Our aims are two-fold. We will determine, first in cellular models and then in vivo, the relative contributions of PKA, Rapgef3/4, and NCS/Rapgef2 to cAMP-dependent signaling driving neuronal activities such as progenitor-cell growth arrest and differentiation, morphological changes including neuritogenesis and dendritic homeostasis, and cytoprotection in response to trauma or stress. Next, we will determine the physiological relevance of the cAMP-NCS/Rapgef2-ERK signaling system to CNS adaptive and maladaptive responses to chronic stress, minor traumatic brain injury, and learning involved in processing of aversive and rewarding stimuli.
</div>

<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Summary
 </div></div>
 
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
The neuropeptide slow transmitter PACAP (pituitary adenylate cyclase activating polypeptide) is released at synapses that transduce stress responses to the brain, and mediate homeostatic adjustments to stress by the organism. Allostatic responses to systemic and psychogenic stressors at multiple points in development and throughout the life span are implicated as causative factors in depression and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). Stress response pathways (resilience responses) may also be required to ameliorate delayed neuronal death (DND) in traumatic brain injury, long-term exposure to intense physical or psychological stimuli, or brain inflammation in chronic neurodegenerative disease. Understanding the cellular mechanisms of stress transduction is crucial to developing effective therapeutic interventions for these disorders.<br /><br />In completing the first part of our long-term goal to identify the specific contributions of the three major cAMP sensors PKA, Epac (Rapgef2 3 and 4), and NCS/Rapgef2 to major cAMP-dependent processes carried out by neuronal cells, we have demonstrated in the NS-1 neuroendocrine cell line that PKA (and not Epac or NCS/Rapgef2) is required for cAMP-dependent cell survival upon serum withdrawal; that Epac (and not PKA or NCS/Rapgef2) is required for cAMP-dependent growth arrest; and that NCS/Rapgef2 (and not PKA or Epac) is required for cAMP-initiated neuritogenesis (A.C. Emery, M.V. Eiden and L.E. Eiden, J.Biol. Chem. 289: 10126, 2014). Further efforts are underway to show that the concept of parcellation of these three cAMP pathways also applies to neurons and neuronal progenitor cells of the central nervous system. <br /><br />Progress towards completion of our second major goal of demonstrating a physiological role for the cAMP-NCS/Rapgef2-ERK signaling cassette in the central nervous system has involved, as a first step, an inventory of the Gs-coupled GPCRs capable of signaling through this pathway. At this writing, five GPCRs tested appear to signal through engagement of NCS/Rapgef2, while two do not, indicating that cAMP-NCS/Rapgef2-ERK signaling is likely to be a property of most, but not all, Gs-coupled GPCRs.<br /><br />
