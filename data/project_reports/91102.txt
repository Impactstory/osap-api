<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Goals and Objectives
 </div></div>
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
The goal of this research program is to understand how frontal cortex interacts with the striatum to produce goal-directed behavior under normal and pathological conditions. This neural system, often referred to as the frontal-striatal system, is also strongly modulated by dopamine, serotonin and other neuromodulators, and therefore we also examine the effects of these neuromodulators on behaviors mediated by this system. Extensive work has implicated frontal-striatal systems and the dopamine modulation of these systems in psychiatric disorders, particularly schizophrenia. Schizophrenia represents a major public health burden with approximately 2.4 million American adults, or about 1.1% of the population age 18 and older in a given year, suffering from this disease. One of the leading hypotheses of the pathology that underlies schizophrenia is known as the dopamine hypothesis. This hypothesis suggests that schizophrenia is caused by increased dopamine levels in the striatum, and decreased dopamine levels in prefrontal cortex. Although the hypothesis was originally motivated by data which showed that anti-psychotic medication used to treat schizophrenia blocks dopamine transmission, particularly at dopamine D2 receptors which are enriched in the striatum, it received further support recently by experiments that showed that patients exhibited excessive dopamine release under some experimental conditions. <br />Although there is good evidence supporting the dopamine hypothesis in schizophrenia, it remains a matter of debate. The loss of dopamine in the striatum in patients with Parkinsons disease (PD), is, however, well established. Therefore, examining the effects of medication in patients with PD can provide insight into how dopamine levels in the striatum affect cognition. Studies in patients with PD suggest that if dopamine levels are elevated in the striatum subjects learn more from positive feedback and less from negative feedback in tasks in which they must make decisions based upon feedback about past actions. This suggests that patients with schizophrenia may also learn more from positive feedback and less from negative feedback. Ongoing behavioral studies in patients have begun to examine this hypothesis. This work is conducted under protocol 13-M-0190, NCT01950377.<br /><br />In conjunction with the clinical work, we are also interested in what frontal-striatal systems contribute to normal behavior. Understanding their contribution to normal behavior can help elucidate why pathology in these systems gives rise to the symptoms of the disorders. We work on the hypothesis that some components of these systems are important in decision making. This hypothesis is motivated by the fact that one of the side-effects of the medications used to treat PD is a change in the way patients make decisions. Some patients with PD develop behavioral addictions, and these addictions are more common in patients treated with dopamine agonists, which is one of the classes of drugs used to treat the motor symptoms. Therefore one of our goals is to understand which patients might develop these addictions, and what changes in the circuits when the patients develop the addictions. Behavioral addictions are related to impulsivity, and impulsivity is a measure of how much immediate reward is traded for longer-term rewards. Therefore, we study the role of frontal-striatal circuits, and dopamine modulation in these circuits, in the trade-off between immediate and long-term rewards.<br />
</div>
</div>

<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Summary
 </div></div>
 
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
Our lab uses a combination of neurophysiology, pharmacology and functional imaging to examine the role of frontal striatal systems in behavior. Several of our experiments are motivated by studies in patient groups that show that patients have deficits on particular tasks. We then study those tasks in the lab to delineate the neural circuits that underlie the behaviors, as well as how brain areas within those neural circuits interact to drive the behavior. Recent work has focused on understanding the mechanisms that drive the choice of novel options in decision making tasks. We use tasks in which participants select particular options and learn the reward properties of each of those options. We then introduce novel choice options at stochastic intervals. We have found that increasing dopamine levels, by blocking dopamine reuptake, can increase preference for novel options. We have also found that the amygdala plays a role in preference for novel objects, in combination with orbital frontal cortex and the ventral striatum. In related experiments in the lab, we have been studying action selection, when actions must be selected under different rules. We have found that both lateral prefrontal cortex and the dorsal striatum play a role in this process. Further, lateral prefrontal cortex appears to select actions before the dorsal striatum, when actions are selected on the basis of immediately available information. When actions have to be selected on the basis of past outcomes, however, the dorsal striatum appears to play a more important role. Thus, these two interconnected brain structures appear to play complimentary roles in selecting actions.
</div>
