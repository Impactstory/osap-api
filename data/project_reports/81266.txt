<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Goals and Objectives
 </div></div>
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
The goal of this research program is to understand how frontal cortex interacts with the striatum to produce goal directed behavior under normal and pathological conditions.  This system, often referred to as the frontal-striatal system, is also strongly modulated by dopamine, serotonin and other neuromodulators, and therefore we also examine the effects of these neuromodulators on behaviors mediated by this system.  Extensive work has implicated frontal-striatal systems and the dopamine modulation of these systems in psychiatric disorders, particularly schizophrenia.  Schizophrenia  represents a major public health burden with approximately 2.4 million American adults, or about 1.1% of the population age 18 and older in a given year, suffering from this disease.  One of the leading hypotheses of the pathology that underlies schizophrenia, is known as the dopamine hypothesis.  This hypothesis suggests that schizophrenia is caused by increased dopamine levels in the striatum, and decreased dopamine levels in prefrontal cortex.  Although the hypothesis was originally motivated by data which showed that anti-psychotic medication used to treat schizophrenia blocks dopamine transmission, particularly at dopamine D2 receptors which are enriched in the striatum, it received further support recently by experiments that showed that patients exhibited excessive dopamine release under some experimental conditions.  <br />	Although there is good evidence supporting the dopamine hypothesis in schizophrenia, it remains a matter of debate.  The loss of dopamine in the striatum in patients with Parkinsons disease (PD), is, however, well established.  Therefore, examining the effects of medication in patients with PD can provide insight into how dopamine levels in the striatum affect cognition.  Studies in patients with Parkinson disease suggest that if dopamine levels are elevated in the striatum, as is hypothesized to be the case in schizophrenia, subjects learn more from positive feedback and less from negative feedback in tasks in which they must make decisions based upon feedback about past actions.  This work suggests that patients with schizophrenia may also learn more from positive feedback and less from negative feedback.  Ongoing behavioral studies in patients have begun to examine this hypothesis.  <br />In conjunction with the clinical work, we are also interested in what frontal-striatal systems contribute to normal behavior.  Understanding their contribution to normal behavior can help elucidate why pathology in these systems give rise to the symptoms of the disorders.  We work on the hypothesis that some components of these systems are important in decision making.  This hypothesis is motivated by the fact that one of the side-effects of the medications used to treat Parkinson disease is a change in the way patients make decisions.  Some patients with PD develop behavioral addictions, and these addictions are more common in patients treated with dopamine agonists, which is one of the classes of drugs used to treat the motor symptoms.  Therefore one of our goals is to understand which patients might develop these addictions, and what changes in the circuits when the patients develop the addictions.  Behavioral addictions are related to impulsivity, and impulsivity is a measure of how much immediate reward is traded for longer-term rewards.  Therefore, we study the role of frontal-striatal circuits, and dopamine modulation in these circuits, in the trade-off between immediate and long-term rewards.<br />
</div>
</div>

<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Summary
 </div></div>
 
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
Our lab is engaged in collaborations with clinical groups to examine behavior in patients with schizophrenia and Parkinsons disease, using tasks which engage frontal-striatal systems to see if patients show deficits in these tasks.  Our strategy is to identify behavioural deficits in patient groups, and then to pursue those deficits using neuroscientific tools, to try to understand the mechanisms that underlie the behavioral differences in groups.  Specifically, we carry out behavioural studies in patient groups to identify tasks that differentiate patient and control groups.  Once these behaviours have been identified, we can use neuroimaging to identify the brain regions that are responding differently in the control and patients groups.  We can also use pharmacological manipulations in control groups to try to modify their behaviour to match the patient group.  Following that, more detailed techniques can be used to try to examine the circuit mechanisms that underlie the differences in behaviour between the groups.  The ultimate goal is to examine the effects of neuromodulators at the micro-circuit level, and to link changes in processing at the microcircuit level to changes in behaviour, to help elucidate the pathology that underlies schizophrenia, Parkinson's disease and related disorders.
</div>
