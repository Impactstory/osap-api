<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Goals and Objectives
 </div></div>
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
This project is focused on the genetics of the response to general anesthetics. General anesthesia is not only a vital tool for the practice of surgery but also a potentially powerful resource for the exploration of the nervous system. Anesthetized patients are sedated, have depressed motor and autonomic responses to painful stimuli, and do not form memory traces of ongoing events. Nevertheless, a variety of monitoring techniques demonstrate that significant neural activity persists during anesthesia; it is the general level of arousal that is depressed. Thus, genes that influence general anesthesia could help to identify the molecular and cellular substrates that underlie arousal and the loss of consciousness that accompanies its depression. Such genes are likely to be of interest to the field of mental health. Not only has recent work demonstrated the efficacy of the general anesthetic ketamine in the treatment of depression, there is a growing awareness that alteration in the synchrony of brain waves, a hallmark of general anesthesia, is commonly associated with schizophrenia. Thus, identification of genes that influence anesthesia may provide strong candidates for genetic studies of mental illness. <br /><br />To find such genes, we use Drosophila because it has a desirable combination of simple husbandry and a complex nervous system. Our early work established that Drosophila and vertebrates have similar sensitivities to a broad array of volatile anesthetics, suggesting that the molecular targets are conserved. We also showed that at least one neural circuit continued to function even though anesthetics had rendered the fly immobile and unresponsive. Thus, just as in vertebrates, the Drosophila nervous system appears to have elements with differential sensitivity to anesthetics. Further, as in mammals, mutations in candidate anesthetic target genes alter the effects of different volatile anesthetic compounds to varying extents, indicating that each target is preferentially sensitive to a subset of drugs.  Taken together, these facts led us to conclude that the fruit fly was a proper model organism for this study.  We have used a combination of behavioral and cell biological assays to identify genetic loci that are important for anesthesia sensitivity and determine how individual genes influence the anesthetic responsiveness of specific neural pathways and individual neuronal components.
</div>

<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Summary
 </div></div>
 
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
The Section on Molecular Genetics closed this year and the final two members of the Section, Staff Scientist Dr. David Sandstrom and postdoctoral fellow Elyssa Burg, both moved on to new positions after four months. Both of them concluded their respective research projects and brought them to publication prior to leaving. A paper by Dr. Sandstrom and several co-authors describes evidence that the Drosophila ryanodine receptor is a target of volatile anesthetics and was published in the journal Anesthesiology (Gao et al., 2013, Anesthesiology 118: 587-601). A paper by Dr. Burg and several co-authors characterized the molecular and cellular basis of a simple social behavior in Drosophila (i.e. Resource Independent Local Enhancement) and was published in the journal Genes, Brain and Behavior (Burg et al., 2013, Genes, Brain, and Behavior 12: 338-47). These two papers  and the FY2012 Annual Report should be consulted for a summary of the final research conducted by the SOMG.
