<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Goals and Objectives
 </div></div>
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
In susceptible subjects, stress can lead to prolonged negative emotional states and pathological conditions such as anxiety disorders and major depression, both of which impair quality of life and are prevalent in society. Unfortunately, current strategies for treating mood disorders have produced low remission rates (e.g., 30% for major depressive disorder). This highlights the urgency of identifying the precise mechanisms by which the brain processes stress information to impact behavior, in order to uncover more effective treatment strategies. The Unit on the Neurobiology of Affective Memory is addressing this gap by testing the prediction that the paraventricular nucleus of the thalamus (PVT), a structure that is readily activated by both physical and psychological stressors, determines stress susceptibility and controls both adaptive (appropriate) and maladaptive (inappropriate) behavioral responses to stress. To achieve its scientific mission our unit incorporates an interdisciplinary approach, which includes: animal behavior analysis, electrophysiology, optogenetics, mouse genetics and imaging.
</div>

<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Summary
 </div></div>
 
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
This fiscal year we successfully launched our research program aimed at studying the role of the PVT in stress processing and the control of stress susceptibility. This included designing and setting up laboratory equipment, hiring and training laboratory staff, and conducting pilot studies. To begin investigating the role of PVT in stress processing, we first designed and implemented a behavioral protocol that reliably measures stress memory across many different mouse strains and genetic lines. Second, we identified putative cellular substrates of stress in PVT some of which are likely to be associated with stress-induced chances in gene expression.  Finally, we drafted and published a review article that summarizes current literature on the neuronal pathways controlling the retrieval of aversive memories and proposed a model supporting the involvement of PVT in this process. 
</div>
