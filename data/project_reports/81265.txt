<div class="data">
Project 1: Enkephalinergic compounds for major depressive disorder (Anxious depression)<br /><br />Primary aim<br />     The primary aim of the study is to determine if the highly selective delta-opioid receptor agonist AZD2327 is effective in the treatment of AMDD as assessed by the proportion who show a clinically significant change from baseline to endpoint in the Hamilton Rating Scale for Depression (HRSD17).<br />Hypothesis:  Adult subjects with AMDD without psychotic features when randomized to acute therapy with the highly selective delta-opioid receptor agonist AZD2327 will have a significantly greater proportion of responders from baseline to week 4 in the HRSD17 and HAM-A total scores compared to subjects randomized to placebo.<br /><br />Project 2: An investigation to determine whether levels of p11 protein in peripheral blood cells correlate with treatment response to citalopram in patients with major depressive disorder.<br />Hypothesis:  Changes in p11 levels of peripheral blood cells in patients with major depressive disorder will correlate with treatment response to citalopram. <br /><br />Project 3: Identifying biomarkers of diagnosis, illness and treatment response. Using data from the repository protocol from studies conducted in the Mood and Anxiety Disorders (MAP) which includes genetics, neuropsychological testing, structural and brain imaging, electrophysiological studies and peripheral blood measures were are examining markers of diagnosis and treatment response.<br />Project 4. Target validation of novel treatments in mood and anxiety disorders<br />A: A selective NR2B antagonist in patient depression<br />Hypothesis: Subjects with treatment-resistant major depressive disorder who are treated with an oral selective NR2B antagonist will have a rapid antidepressant response.<br /><br />B: A low-trapping NMDA channel blocker in patient depression<br />Hypothesis: Subjects with treatment-resistant major depressive disorder who are treated with a a low-trapping NMDA antagonist intravenously will have a rapid antidepressant response.<br /><br />Results in the past year:<br />1) We found an anxiolytic but not antidepressant signal with a highly selective delta-opioid receptor agonist in patients with anxious depression.<br />2) We found that P11 is low in depressed patients and increases with treatment with serotonin-reuptake inhibitors. Additional analyses are being completed.<br />3) Identifying biomarkers of diagnosis, illness and treatment response<br />a. Our collaborators and us developed a population-based input function and image-derived input function for (11)C(R)-rolipram imaging in major depressive disorder. We found that cAMP as determined by (11)C(R)-rolipram imaging is low in depressed compared to healthy controls and that cAMP increases with serotonin-reuptake inhibitors (a first-line antidepressant). This suggests that PDE4 an enzyme within this cAMP cascade may be a target for developing novel antidepressants.<br />b. We found that manic episodes transmit independently than major depressive episodes in families of individuals with mood disorders. This suggests that different approaches in the study of genetics might need to take place in order to determine the genetic underpinnings in mood disorders.<br />e.  Our collaborators and us conducted a developmental studies to examine the neural circuitry mediating motor inhibition and response to faces in bipolar disorder across the lifespan.<br />Project 4A: <br />We found that the NR2B antagonist MK-0657 has rapid antidepressant effects without the dissociative side effects of ketamine. As a result of our work, Cerecor has licensed this compound from Merck. <br />We found that the low-trapping NMDA antagonist AZD6765 produces rapid antidepressant effects without dissociative side effects. As a result of our work, Astrazeneca is pursuing the indication of treatment-resistant depression with this compound.<br />

<div class="rowdiv">
<div class="headings">
Summary
 </div></div>
 
 <div class="rowdiv">
<div class="data">
Project 1: Enkephalinergic compounds for major depression: There is increasing evidence that patients with anxious major depressive disorder (AMDD) have a greater depressive severity, functional impairment, increased risk of suicidality, worse social distress, higher incidence of alcohol and drug abuse, and poorer treatment response and outcome than patients with non-anxious depression. There is increasing literature of the involvement of the endogenous opioid system in major depression and its treatment. Identification of delta-opioid receptor as a possible target in the treatment of depression and anxiety began with clinical observations that a heightened anxiety state and depressive-like behaviors were consistently noted in the delta-opioid receptor knockout mouse. A number of investigators have found that selective delta-opioid receptor agonists have antidepressant-like properties in models such as the forced swim test. In a search for a selective delta-opioid receptor agonist to test in a proof-of-concept clinical study in AMDD, AZD2327 is a potent, first in class, high-affinity enkephalinergic agonist that possesses anxiolytic and antidepressant activity in animal models. AZD2327 has efficacy comparable to diazepam and imipramine in rodent models of anxiety and depression, respectively. Phase I studies have been completed and have indicated an acceptable safety profile.  Male and female patients, ages 18 to 65, with a diagnosis of major depression (without psychotic features) meeting criteria for AMDD, will be randomized to double-blind treatment to receive either AZD2327 (3 mg BID) or placebo in a 2:1 ratio for a period of 4 weeks. In addition, a series of surrogate neurobiological markers will be obtained to establish whether they are capable of predicting therapeutic response. Approximately 96 patients with acute major depression will be enrolled in the study.<br /><br />Project 2: An investigation to determine whether levels of p11 protein in peripheral blood cells correlate with treatment response to citalopram in patients with major depressive disorder.<br />Major depressive disorder (MDD) is a serious, debilitating, life-shortening illness that affects many persons of all ages and backgrounds.  While treatments are effective for a significant portion of patients with MDD, progress in developing more effective treatments is lagging. Furthermore, with regards to existing antidepressant medications, there are yet no reliable predictors of the likelihood of remission, response or non-response with an initial trial of an antidepressant medication. Identifying factors that are likely to predict response would have the advantage of personalizing treatment to a particular individual; that is, selecting the antidepressant medication that is most likely to give the greatest probability of having a favorable outcome. <br />	The serotonin system has been implicated in the pathophysiology of depression and mechanism of action ofexisting effective antidepressant treatments. Fourteen different serotonin receptors have been identified to date. One of them, 5-HT1B, plays an important role in regulating serotonin neurotransmission. Recently, p11 (a member of the S100 family of proteins) was found to interact with 5-HT1B receptors (Svenningsson et al 2006; Svenningsson and Greengard 2007). p11 mRNA levels are markedly reduced in the forebrain in helpless H/Rouen mice and the level of p11 mRNA was down-regulated in the anterior cingulate cortex from depressed patients. p11 mRNA is distributed in an anatomical pattern that closely resembled that of 5-HT1B receptor mRNA, including cortex, hippocampus, hypothalamus and raphe nuclei. Chronic administration of the antidepressants imipramine, tranylcypromine, and citalopram significantly increase the level of p11 in cortex. Finally, we have found that chronic treatment with fluoxetine increases p11 in peripheral mononuclear cells in monkeys. <br />	We will now study whether the blood cell levels of p11 differ between healthy individuals and patients suffering from unipolar depression. Moreover, we will study whether the levels of p11 are affected by treatment with the selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor, citalopram. Complementary work will continue at other laboratories to better characterize the role of p11 in the pathophysiology of depression (e.g., animal studies, post-mortem studies).<br />	In addition, we will also acquire a battery of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans in a subset of 45 more homogeneous depressed subjects, and 45 matched healthy controls at baseline and at 8 weeks.  There is a growing body of evidence implicating morphometric and physiologic abnormalities, measureable by MRI, in the pathophysiology of major depressive disorder.   We will assess both baseline differences between depressed subjects and healthy controls, treatment effects, and search for possible MRI markers predicting treatment response.   <br />This is an open label study which will be performed at the National Institute of Mental Health.  In all, 82 adult subjects with major depressive disorder, between the ages of 18 and 65 years, will be recruited from the community.   In addition, we will perform p11 measurements in blood cells from 64 healthy control subjects.<br /><br />Project 3: Identifying biomarkers of diagnosis, illness and treatment response. Using data from the repository protocol from studies conducted in the Mood and Anxiety Disorders (MAP) which includes genetics, neuropsychological testing, structural and brain imaging, electrophysiological studies and peripheral blood measures were are examining markers of diagnosis and treatment response.<br /><br />Project 4. Target validation of novel treatments in mood and anxiety disorders<br />a. NR2B antagonist in treatment-resistant major depressive disorder<br />b. Low-trapping NMDA channel blocker in treatment-resistant major depressive disorder.
